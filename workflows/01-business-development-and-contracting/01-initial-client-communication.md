# 01 — Initial Client Communication

## Objectives
In the first client meeting: demonstrate professionalism, build trust, diagnose core issues, and gather sufficient information to advance subsequent work.

## Prerequisites
- Client has agreed to meet (in-person / online)
- Basic client information is known (company name / industry / scale / attendee titles)

## Inputs (Pre-Meeting Preparation)
- [ ] Client industry research report (at minimum, understand top 3 industry trends and challenges)
- [ ] Client company public information (annual reports / news / funding / org structure)
- [ ] Attendee LinkedIn / background check (understand what they care about)
- [ ] Curated project cases from similar industry/scale (2-3)

---

## Operation Steps

### Step 1: 48 Hours Before Meeting — Prepare Question List

**Structured Question List (ask in order):**

```
Group 1: Context Building — Opening 15-20 minutes

1. "We'd like to first understand your company's overall situation — core business, development priorities over the past 3 years, and what you see as the biggest opportunities and challenges in the next 2-3 years?"
   → Purpose: Understand the business landscape, assess strategic priorities
   → Key to record: What anxiety do their word choices around "opportunities" and "challenges" reveal?

2. "What is the current size and structure of the IT/technology team? What business areas does it primarily support?"
   → Purpose: Understand IT's positioning in the organization (cost center vs. business partner vs. innovation engine)
   → Key to record: Reporting line (does the CIO report to the CEO or CFO?)

3. "What major technology investments or changes have been made in the past two years?"
   → Purpose: Understand investment direction and historical experience (successes/failures)
   → Key to record: Which projects did they spend money on, and which were halted?

Group 2: Pain Discovery — 20-30 minutes

4. "What is the most headache-inducing technology/digitalization issue you're personally dealing with right now?"
   → Purpose: Find pain at the personal level (this is often more real than "organizational goals")
   → Key to record: What are their KPIs? What makes them look bad in front of their boss?

5. "If this issue is not resolved, what impact will it have on the business?"
   → Purpose: Quantify the urgency and importance of the problem
   → Key to record: This answer can be used directly in the subsequent proposal

6. "Have you tried to solve this problem before? What happened?"
   → Purpose: Understand past attempts and root causes, avoid repeating mistakes
   → Key to record: Was the previous approach "the wrong solution" or "poor execution"?

Group 3: Expectation Setting — 10-15 minutes

7. "Assuming we can help you solve this problem, what outcome would you consider a 'success'?"
   → Purpose: Define success criteria and quantifiable KPIs
   → Key to record: Ensure the definition of success is clear before the project begins

8. "In terms of decision-making process, which key stakeholders need to support this project? What is the approximate budget range?"
   → Purpose: Understand decision process and budget reality
   → Key to record: Who is the ultimate decision-maker? How long does budget approval take?

9. "If we provide an initial recommendation later, what format would be most helpful to you? Report / PPT / workshop?"
   → Purpose: Understand their communication preferences
   → Key to record: Cater to their preferences
```

### Step 2: During the Meeting — Execute 4 Key Behaviors

**Behavior 1: The 80/20 Rule — Client speaks 80%, you speak 20%**
- The core goal of the first meeting is to "listen," not to "talk"
- Only ask questions when the client needs guidance; don't interrupt
- Keep your speaking time under 20% of total meeting time

**Behavior 2: In the final 3 minutes — deliver an "instant insight"**
```
Before the meeting ends, find a moment to say:
"Based on our discussion, I have a few preliminary observations that may be useful to you —"
Then deliver 1-2 high-quality observations.

Examples:
"I noticed you mentioned System X was implemented 5 years ago, but that vendor was acquired last year — that could be a risk point."
"The 'business units buying their own SaaS' phenomenon you described is actually quite common in manufacturing — the key isn't prohibition, but establishing lightweight governance — I've seen similar cases resolved in 3 steps."

This "instant insight" is the most effective way to build trust:
- Proves you understood
- Proves you have experience
- Gives them a signal that "this meeting was worth it"
```

**Behavior 3: Define next steps clearly — never end with a vague "we'll be in touch"**
```
Before the meeting ends, must confirm:
"Thank you very much for your time. Here's what we'll do next —
1) [Specific action], expected by [time];
2) [Specific action], we'll need your help with [what].
Does that sound reasonable?"
```

**Behavior 4: Don't over-promise beyond your boundaries**
```
If the client asks about something you cannot/should not do:
"That area involves [legal / financial audit / ...]; my expertise is in [IT strategy / technology / ...].
I'd recommend bringing in a professional [lawyer / accountant / ...] to evaluate that — I can help you outline the questions you'd need to ask them."
This actually enhances professional credibility.
```

### Step 3: Within 24 Hours After Meeting — Send Meeting Minutes and Next Steps

**Meeting Minutes Template:**

```markdown
# Meeting Minutes — [Client Company] × [Your Company]

**Date**: 202X-XX-XX
**Attendees**: [Client side] / [Our side]
**Meeting Purpose**: Initial discussion to understand client IT/digitalization needs

## Key Information Summary
1. [Core business and strategic priorities]
2. [Current IT/digitalization capability overview]
3. [3-5 key pain points identified]
4. [Client's definition of "success"]

## Our Preliminary Observations
1. [Observation 1 — MECE category: Technology / Process / Organization / Data]
2. [Observation 2]
3. [Observation 3]

## Recommended Next Steps
1. [Specific action 1] — Our responsibility, expected by [time]
2. [Specific action 2] — Client assistance needed, deadline [time]
3. [Specific action 3] — Proposed next meeting [time / agenda]

## Future Possibilities (no commitment, for reference)
- [Possible project direction 1]
- [Possible project direction 2]
```

## Outputs Quality Checklist

Pre-meeting preparation:
- [ ] Company public information reviewed (annual reports / news / funding)
- [ ] Attendee backgrounds researched
- [ ] Structured question list prepared (9 questions covering 3 groups)
- [ ] 2-3 relevant cases prepared

Post-meeting:
- [ ] Meeting minutes sent within 24 hours
- [ ] Minutes include "instant insight" content
- [ ] Next steps and responsible parties clearly defined
- [ ] Client replied confirming receipt or expressed positive feedback

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Meeting objectives were documented in writing before the meeting (sent to client)
- [ ] Three-phase question list prepared, with 3-5 specific questions per phase
- [ ] Client industry background researched in advance (at least 1 hour of research)
- [ ] Your speaking time during meeting <40%, client speaking >60%
- [ ] Client said "that's a good question" at least twice
- [ ] Meeting minutes sent within 24 hours
- [ ] Minutes include clear next steps (who / what / when)
- [ ] Minutes include "instant insight" — making the client feel "this consultant did their homework"

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Correct Approach |
|------|---------|
| Pitching your solution in the first meeting | The first meeting is for listening and diagnosis; solutions come next time |
| Telling the client "this is easy" | Even if you think it's easy, say "needs assessment" — making light of it makes people doubt your professionalism |
| Client asks "have you worked in this industry?" and you say "no but..." | Honestly say no, but immediately add "we've done X in similar scenarios" or "the methodology is cross-industry" |
| Over-promising to win the project | Clear boundaries > winning the project and then failing to deliver |
| No one taking notes during the meeting | Designate one person to take minutes, not "write from memory afterwards" |
