# 04 — SOW Contract & Negotiation

## Objectives
Transform the promises in the Proposal into a legally binding Statement of Work (SOW), protect both parties' interests, define delivery standards clearly, and drive toward signing.

## Prerequisites
- Proposal has received positive client feedback
- Both parties are broadly aligned on project scope and methodology
- Budget has been confirmed (at least order of magnitude)

## Inputs
- Approved Proposal
- Client contract template (if the client has one)
- Legal review comments (if available)

## Operation Steps

### Step 1: Standard SOW Structure

```
1. Project Background & Objectives
   [Refined from Proposal executive summary — why this project, definition of success]

2. Scope of Work
   2.1 Service overview
   2.2 Detailed scope of work
   2.3 Out of Scope
   2.4 Assumptions

3. Deliverables & Acceptance Criteria
   [Each deliverable: name + description + format + acceptance criteria + acceptance timeframe]

4. Timeline & Milestones
   [Including milestones dependent on the client]

5. Team & Responsibilities
   [Both teams + roles + responsibilities + allocation percentage]

6. Fees & Payment Schedule
   [Total price / billing method / payment milestones / what is and is not included]

7. Change Control
   [Change request submission process / evaluation / approval / fee adjustment mechanism]

8. Governance
   [SteerCo / weekly meetings / escalation path / communication mechanisms]

9. Terms & Conditions
   [Confidentiality / IP / non-compete / termination / dispute resolution / limitation of liability]

10. Signature Page
```

### Step 2: Writing Acceptance Criteria — This Determines Project Success

Bad (not measurable): "Complete the IT strategy planning report"
Good (measurable + verifiable):

```
Deliverable: IT Strategy Planning Report

Acceptance Criteria:
1. Report covers the following chapters: Strategic Context / Current State Assessment /
   Target Blueprint / Gap Analysis / Investment Portfolio / 3-Year Roadmap /
   Governance / Financial Model (8 chapters total)
2. Executive summary no more than 2 pages, readable as a standalone document
3. Roadmap includes at least 3 phases (Q1-Q2 / Q3-Q6 / Q7-Q12+) with milestones per phase
4. Financial model includes 5-year TCO forecast and NPV/IRR analysis,
   with optimistic / baseline / pessimistic scenarios
5. Maturity scores supported by industry benchmark data

Delivery Format: PDF + editable PPT source files
Acceptance Timeframe: Client to confirm in writing or submit revision requests
                      within 10 business days of receipt;
                      no response within this period = deemed accepted
Revision Cap: 1 round of major revisions (structure / conclusions) +
              1 round of detail revisions (wording / formatting)
```

### Step 3: Fee Negotiation Strategy

**Responding when the client pushes back on price:**

```
Client says: "Too expensive — the other firm quoted half your price"

Don't: Immediately drop the price (this makes it look like you were inflating the price from the start)

Instead:
Step 1 (Understand): "I understand budget is important to you. Would you mind sharing whether their scope matches ours?"
Step 2 (Align): "We may include more than they do in the following areas: [X] [Y] [Z].
            If we adjust these scope items, we can optimize cost without sacrificing core value."
Step 3 (Options): "We can offer three choices:
   Option A (Full scope): $X — covers all requirements
   Option B (Core scope): $0.7X — covers the most critical parts, remainder executed by client team
   Option C (Lightweight diagnosis + directional recommendations): $0.3X — rapid diagnosis + strategic direction, no detailed solution"
```

**Three "Don'ts" of Pricing Concessions:**
- Don't concede on the first round of negotiation
- Don't lower price without adjusting scope (every price reduction corresponds to a scope adjustment)
- Don't reduce by more than 20% (anything more suggests the original quote was inflated, damaging credibility for future engagements)

### Step 4: Contract Red Lines — These Clauses Are Non-Negotiable

```
Absolutely Unacceptable:
  ❌ Unlimited Liability clauses
     → Must set a cap, typically 1-2x the contract value
     → Talking point: "Industry standard is a reasonable cap on liability, protecting both parties"

  ❌ Full IP Assignment without limits
     → Your methodology / frameworks / tools are core assets, not a one-time sell-off
     → Talking point: "Project-specific deliverables created for this engagement belong to the client,
        but the methodologies and tools we bring to the project are licensed for client use, with ownership retained by us"

  ❌ Unreasonable payment terms (e.g., 100% payment after project completion and acceptance)
     → Required: 30% on signing + 40% on milestones + 30% on acceptance
     → Talking point: "Milestone-based payment is industry standard, protecting both parties' commitment to project cadence"

  ❌ Unlimited non-compete clauses (Non-compete that blocks too much)
     → Acceptable: Not providing the same type of service to direct competitors during the project
     → Unacceptable: Not serving any client in the same industry for 2 years after project completion

  ❌ One-sided termination rights
     → Both parties should have termination rights, with notice periods and payment for completed work
```

### Step 5: Techniques to Drive Toward Signing

```
1. Reverse Timeline Method:
   "Considering you'd like to see initial results in Q3, we recommend completing the signing
   within [2 weeks], so the project can kick off in [3 weeks], just in time for [key milestone date]."
   (Creates gentle time urgency, without pressure)

2. Interim Deliverable Method:
   "While the contract is going through your process, I can start preparing the draft project
   charter and stakeholder interview list — so we can launch on Day 1 after signing,
   saving you 2-3 weeks."
   (Demonstrates your willingness to invest, building reciprocity psychology)

3. Risk Warning Method:
   "Frankly, the biggest risk to this project is not our approach — it's the start date.
   Every month of delay means [specific impact and dollar amount]."
   (Framed from the client's interest, not your sales quota)

4. Small-Scope-First Method:
   If signing is stuck on a particular point (e.g., client unsure if scope is right):
   "We can start with a 2-week rapid diagnostic phase, produce initial findings,
   then define the subsequent scope. This lets you verify our methodology and fit
   with a small investment."
```

---

## Outputs

- Formal SOW document (signable version)
- Internal pricing approval record
- Negotiation strategy and red-line document (internal use)

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every SOW deliverable has clear acceptance criteria (measurable)
- [ ] Out of Scope section is as clear as In Scope
- [ ] Client dependencies (e.g., "client provides X data within 3 weeks") are explicitly stated
- [ ] Payment milestones are tied to actual delivery milestones
- [ ] Contract red-line clauses have been reviewed
- [ ] Legal review completed (if needed)
- [ ] Both parties' sponsors have confirmed core SOW terms

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Correct Approach |
|------|---------|
| SOW written too vaguely: "Provide IT strategy consulting services" | Specific to deliverable level: "Produce IT strategy planning report (including X chapters, Y diagrams, Z models)" |
| Accepting unreasonable terms to close the deal | Bad terms don't disappear just because you ignore them — they explode when the project hits trouble |
| Out of Scope is only 2-3 lines | Write out 15-20 items that are NOT included. The client forgetting but righteously saying "I thought that was included" = the beginning of scope creep |
| "We'll talk about it later" pricing signals | If you lower the price now, the client remembers "so the price was negotiable" — and will cut it again next time |
