# Executive & Board Presentation

## Objectives

Deliver impactful presentations to the Board / CEO / SteerCo and other executive-level audiences, drive decisions, demonstrate progress, and manage expectations.

---

## Prerequisites
- Presentation purpose is clear (request decision / demonstrate progress / risk alert / project closure)
- Understand the audience (who is attending / what do they care about / what preconceptions exist)
- Data and analysis prepared

## Inputs
- Project progress / results data
- Financial analysis (business case / ROI / TCO)
- Risk and issue log
- Decision request list

---

## Operation Steps

### Step 1: Presentation Planning — Know Your Audience

#### 1.1 Executive Audience Analysis

| Role | Cares About | Fears | How to Address in Presentation |
|------|---------|--------|-------------|
| CEO | Strategic alignment / competitiveness / growth / stock price | Falling behind competitors / missing trends / big investments with no results | Start from industry trends, explain strategic logic, build confidence |
| CFO | Money / returns / risk / cash flow | Investment wasted / budget out of control / unclear ROI | Numbers must be solid, sensitivity analysis ready, alternatives clearly explained |
| COO | Operational impact / efficiency / stability | Business disruption / efficiency decline / team instability | Clearly explain operational impact and transition plan |
| Independent Director | Governance / compliance / risk | Dereliction risk / being uninformed / being held accountable | Demonstrate governance structure and risk management, prove due diligence |
| Business VP | Impact on own department | Loss of control / being replaced / increased burden | 1-on-1 communication beforehand, incorporate their concerns into the plan |

#### 1.2 Pre-Presentation Political Map

Before presenting, you must understand:
- Who supports? (Ensure they publicly support during the meeting)
- Who opposes? (1-on-1 beforehand to understand concerns, adjust plan or prepare responses)
- Who are swing votes? (Communicate individually before the meeting, win support)
- Who is the decision-maker? (Not necessarily the most senior person)

### Step 2: Presentation Structure Design (SCQA Framework)

#### 2.1 SCQA Narrative Framework

McKinsey classic narrative structure:

**S — Situation**
"Our industry is undergoing XXX..."
(Start with facts everyone knows, establish common ground)

**C — Complication**
"But we face XXX challenges/threats/opportunities..."
(Why is the status quo no longer viable? What external changes? What internal issues?)

**Q — Question**
"So, what should we do?"
(Naturally lead to the issues that need discussion and decision)

**A — Answer**
"Our recommendation is XXX"
(Present your solution, supported by data and logic)

#### 2.2 Executive Summary First (BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front)

What executives hate most: listening for 15 minutes and still not knowing what you're trying to say

```
The first 30 seconds of every executive presentation:
"Everyone, today I'm here to present on the XXX project.
Current status is [On Track / At Risk].
I am requesting the Board to approve [1-2 sentence decision request].
If approved, expected [core benefit, 1 number].
I will take [XX] minutes to present, leaving [XX] minutes for Q&A."

Then go into background, logic, and data.
```

### Step 3: Slide-by-Slide Construction

*(See templates/board-presentation-template.md for detailed template)*

#### 3.1 Key Slide Design Principles

**One core message per slide:**
```
❌ Page Title: "Project Progress Update"            (Title is a "topic")
✅ Page Title: "Project on track, three core milestones all completed ahead of schedule" (Title IS the "conclusion")
```

**3-Second Test per slide**: Should know what this slide is saying in a 3-second glance
- Key numbers / conclusions highlighted in large font
- Charts have clear annotations
- Unimportant information goes in appendix

**1-2 Minute per slide principle**:
- Don't spend 8 minutes explaining Slide 5
- If a topic needs deeper discussion, put it in the appendix, open only when asked

#### 3.2 Chart Usage

| What to Express | Which Chart to Use |
|-----------|---------|
| Trend | Line chart |
| Comparison | Bar chart / column chart |
| Composition / Proportion | Stacked bar / pie chart (max 5 slices) / waterfall chart |
| Correlation | Scatter plot |
| Process / Roadmap | Timeline / Gantt chart |
| Distribution | Histogram / box plot |

**Chart Iron Rules:**
- Each chart tells only one story
- Label all axes / units / data sources
- Highlight key data points (color / arrows / annotations)
- Never use 3D charts (distorts data)

### Step 4: High-Risk Scenario Handling

#### 4.1 When You Need to Deliver Bad News

```
Bad News Delivery Framework:

1. [Headline] — State the problem directly
   "Project timeline is delayed by 6 weeks, budget projected to overrun by 15%"

2. [Root Cause] — Root cause (don't make excuses)
   "The root cause is that data migration complexity exceeded our initial estimate:
    we originally thought 5 source systems, actual is 12, and data quality is worse than expected"

3. [Impact] — What is the impact
   "This means Go live moves from Q3 to Q4, total cost increases from $5M to $5.75M"

4. [What We've Done] — What we have already done
   "We have:
    - Added 2 data engineers
    - Adjusted migration strategy (phased instead of big-bang)
    - Implemented automated data quality checks"

5. [Options & Recommendation] — Options and recommendation
   "We now have three options:
    A. Maintain new timeline and budget, risk is medium
    B. Reduce Phase 1 scope, Go live on time and on original budget
    C. Pause and reassess
    We recommend Option B"

6. [Ask] — What is needed
   "We need the Board to approve the scope adjustment for Option B; with approval today we can continue moving forward"
```

**Key Principles:**
- Deliver bad news immediately, don't hide it
- Come with solutions, not just problems
- Don't over-apologize / get defensive — state facts + solutions + request decision

#### 4.2 When Asked a Question You Don't Know How to Answer

```
Wrong approach:
❌ Bluff: "Um, this... roughly... should be... maybe..." → quickly lose trust
❌ Lie: "No problem, everything is under control" → trust hits zero when discovered

Right approach:
✅ "That's an important question. I don't have the exact data at hand right now, and I don't want to give you an unreliable number.
   I will confirm and give you a written response today / within tomorrow."
✅ Then actually respond within the promised timeframe.
```

### Step 5: Decision Requests

#### 5.1 How to Write Decision Requests

Poor decision requests:
"Please approve the project to continue" (too vague)
"Please support our proposal" (support what? Money? People? Resources?)

Good decision requests:
```
Request the Board to approve the following three items:

1. Approve IT strategic direction and 3-year investment framework (total amount $[X]M)
   - This framework is directional; annual specific budgets will return to the Board for separate approval

2. Approve Year 1 investment of $[Y]M to launch the following work:
   - [Initiative 1]: $[A]M
   - [Initiative 2]: $[B]M
   - [Initiative 3]: $[C]M

3. Authorize the CIO with decision-making authority within the following scope (to accelerate execution):
   - Individual changes <$[Z]K AND schedule impact <2 weeks
   - All decisions beyond this scope will be escalated to the Board / SteerCo

Recommend decision by [date] to catch [critical time window, e.g., vendor annual renewal / key talent hiring window].
```

#### 5.2 Time-Sensitive Decisions

If the decision is truly time-sensitive, explain why:
"We need to sign the contract by June 30, because the vendor's annual price increase of 12% takes effect July 1. Missing this window increases Year 1 costs by $[X]M."

## Quality Check
- [ ] First 30 seconds clearly state: what I'm here to do + what decision I'm requesting
- [ ] Every slide title IS the core message of that slide (not a topic label)
- [ ] All numbers have sources and can withstand follow-up questioning
- [ ] Prepared Top 5 most likely questions and answers
- [ ] If presentation includes decision requests, decisions are specific and actionable
- [ ] Pre-meeting 1-on-1 communication with key decision-makers done (no ambushes at Board meetings)
- [ ] Appendix prepared with supporting details (can flip to when asked)

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|---------|---------|
| Too many slides | 20 slides, 40 minutes, audience has forgotten what slide 3 said | 10-15 slides, 1-2 min each, total no more than 20 minutes |
| Data dump without synthesis | "Slide 7 has 12 numbers, each one is important" — result: none remembered | Highlight only 1 key number per slide, rest in appendix |
| Reading slides | Executives can read themselves, they don't need you to read to them | Face the audience and speak; PPT is visual aid, not your teleprompter |
| Not handling politics in advance | First time decision-makers hear your proposal is at the Board meeting, then expect them to approve on the spot | Pre-meeting 1-on-1 communication, resolve concerns, let decision-makers "publicly confirm what was already agreed" |
| Only presenting when things go well | Hide when problems arise, only report when the problem explodes and can't be hidden | Bad news early, good news can be slower |
| Answering the same person repeatedly | A board member keeps asking the same question, you've answered three times | Recognize it and proactively say: "This seems to be a point you particularly care about; let me discuss it with you in depth one-on-one after the meeting" |
